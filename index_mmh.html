Challenges in differential mental disorder detection andour solutions.

Abstract

Differential diagnosis of mental disorders remains a fundamental challenge in real-world clinical practice, where multiple conditions often exhibit overlapping symptoms. However, most existing methods or datasets are developed under single-disorder settings and rely on limited data elicitation paradigms, restricting their ability to capture disorder-specific patterns. In this work, we investigate differential mental disorder detection through psychology-inspired multimodal stimuli, designed to elicit diverse emotional, cognitive, and behavioral responses grounded in findings from experimental psychology. Based on this paradigm, we collect a large-scale multimodal mental health dataset (MMH) covering depression, anxiety, and schizophrenia, with all diagnostic labels clinically verified by licensed psychiatrists. To effectively model the heterogeneous signals induced by different elicitation paradigms, we further propose a paradigm-aware multimodal framework, which leverages prompt-guided semantic descriptions to enhance representation learning across modalities. Extensive experiments show that our framework consistently outperforms existing baselines, under-scoring the value of psychology-grounded stimulus design for differential mental disorder detection. Psychology-Inspired Stimulus Design

The dataset is built around five elicitation tasks designed to cover affective induction, auditory perception, controlled speech, social-emotional cognition, and structured interaction.

Task 1 · Multi-modal Stimulation I (MS-I)

Happy, neutral, sad, and scared affective clips with synchronized image-audio stimuli.

Task 2 · Unimodal Stimulation (US)

Audio-only emotional stimuli across low, middle, and high frequency groups.

Task 3 · Text Reading

Controlled reading of negative-emotion text to capture vocal expressiveness and fluency.

Task 4 · Multi-modal Stimulation II (MS-II)

Positive, neutral, and negative videos for higher-level affective and social cognition.

Task 5 · Human–Computer Interview

15 standardized questions covering emotional state, self-perception, behavioral intention, and anxiety detection.
